When Shawn breaks down, Theo instinctively offers "I'm sorry, buddy" and tries to make him comfortable. Joe respects the impulse but names why it backfires with a deeply enduring person: reassurance reads as weakness, and it makes them feel small — the one thing they can never allow themselves to feel. Promising someone "I can make you safe and comfortable" is something a person in their trauma will never trust.

> "The thing like I'm sorry is again like the shot in like it's weakness instead of like this is strength. What you're doing right now is strong."

Real safety isn't saying "everything's going to be okay." It's the willingness to be with someone in the experience without trying to fix it, run from it, or get too uncomfortable. Often the most powerful response is silent presence — "I got you. I'm right here" — or even no words at all.

For an endurer especially, the medicine is to be witnessed in strength, not pitied in weakness: "I am super proud of you for being so strong and feeling this. I'm right here. You are rocking it." What they need is a safe place to break down and be seen — and crucially, not to be made small in it.
