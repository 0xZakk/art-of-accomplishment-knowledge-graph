Helping someone who hasn't asked for help looks generous but is actually disempowering. Joe's image is sharp: it's like taking on someone's credit card debt without relieving them of it — you just double the debt. You're carrying all their work, but you haven't taken it off their plate.

> "If you're helping somebody without them asking for help, you're basically taking on their credit card debt, but you're not relieving them of it. You're just doubling the credit card debt."

The deeper damage is the message it sends. Unrequested help silently says "I don't believe you can do this on your own. I don't have faith in you. I don't trust you to get it done." It erodes trust and makes the other person feel smaller. Requested help is completely different — when someone asks "can I get a hand?", helping is connection, not a verdict on their competence.

This is why the chronic helper ends up surrounded by resentment. The over-helping boss who wonders why they're alone, the parents who help the teenager with everything and get a despondent, resentful kid in return — all of it is the predictable result of treating people as incapable. The antidote is to hold expectations and let people ask: "Tell me if you can't do it. Tell me if you need help."
