Joe asks the client: "How many attempts would you have to make building an app before you had undeniable proof that you couldn't do it?" The honest answer: one. A single failed attempt would feel like proof of fundamental inability. The client knows intellectually this is irrational — he understands iterative development, failing fast, experimentation — but his emotional system treats the first attempt as a final verdict.

> "I'd like to say many, but probably the honest answer is one."

This creates a paralyzing bind: if one failure proves you can't, then starting carries existential risk. Better to not start than to confirm you're incapable. The "body won't let me" isn't mystical — it's a rational response to an irrational premise. If the stakes are truly "one shot to prove your worth," then anxiety, avoidance, and self-doubt are appropriate responses.

Joe validates the self-doubt: "It's good that you don't think you can do it, because you can't — if that's true." If one failure really does prove inability, then yes, you can't do it, because no one succeeds on the first try. The absurdity cracks the frame open. The problem isn't lack of ability but the impossible standard against which ability is being measured.
