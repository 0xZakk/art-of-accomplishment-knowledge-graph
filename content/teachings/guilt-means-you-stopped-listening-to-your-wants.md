Joe gives guilt a simple, redirecting definition: it means you've stopped listening to your deepest wants. Rather than treating guilt as proof you've done something wrong, he treats it as a signal that you've lost contact with what you actually want.

> "Guilt just means that we've stopped listening to our deepest wants."

From there he offers two practical ways to get back in touch with those wants when guilt shows up. The first is to imagine the guilt removed: "If I couldn't feel guilty right now, what else would I notice? What else do I want? What else do I feel?" Subtracting the guilt lets the underlying desire and feeling become visible.

The second is forward-looking: "How do I want to do that thing differently next time?" This converts the stuck, backward-facing quality of guilt into a concrete intention about the future, reconnecting you with your own preference and agency.

Both moves treat guilt the same way the rest of Joe's emotional map treats every feeling — as a signpost, here pointing back toward the wants you've stopped hearing.
