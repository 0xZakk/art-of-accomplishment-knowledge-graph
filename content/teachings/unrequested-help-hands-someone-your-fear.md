Underneath compulsive helping is fear. The chronic helper is usually scared that something will go wrong or that they'll get in trouble — and instead of feeling that fear themselves, they convert it into action and hand the anxiety to someone else to carry.

> "What you're doing is you're taking all that anxiety, you're just handing it to someone else to hold for you."

Joe illustrates with a man stressing out over building a house while his architect wife is right there. The man's vacation is wrecked and he shows up to work depleted — until Joe asks whether the wife even wanted help, or whether he just stressed out and disempowered her in the process. That's the recognition that cracks the pattern open.

When you strip away the appearance of generosity, the real message of unrequested help is: "Will you hold my fear? I'm scared and I don't trust you to do your work." This is why the helper generates resentment rather than gratitude. The work isn't to help more skillfully but to feel and own your own fear instead of offloading it onto the people you claim to be serving.

> "What you're actually doing is you're saying, 'Hey, will you hold my fear? Hey, I'm scared and I don't trust you to actually do your work.'"
