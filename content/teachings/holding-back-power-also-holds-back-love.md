The man feels a "power inside" he is constantly holding back, and he assumes the thing being held back is anger or aggression. As Joe watches, something else surfaces: the more the man says "I'm not a calm person, I'm just holding back my power," the more he smiles. The held-back power turns out to be inseparable from his joy.

> "How much of your power that you're holding back is actually also your joy. >> Oh 100%."

You can't selectively clamp down on one channel of life force. The same valve that mutes the man's anger mutes his joy, and ultimately his love. What he calls a dangerous power he must contain is really "this energy of love that's just dying to come out." Joe puts the obstacle squarely back on him: the problem isn't the love, it's the holding back.

> "You is trouble doing that. Just to be clear."

When the man finally says "you got to take my love or leave it, I can't hold it back anymore," the power, joy, and love all arrive together—because they were never separate to begin with.
