The most counterintuitive way to sabotage the gratitude practice, Joe says, is to do it in order to get something — to be grateful so that you'll get money, or love. The moment you're checking whether it "worked," you've smuggled the lack back in. Wanting the practice to produce the thing assumes you don't have the thing, which reaffirms the exact self-definition you were trying to dissolve.

> "If you're trying to get somewhere, you're still at lack. And if you're still at lack, you're still defining yourself that way and the practice won't work."

The point of the practice is not acquisition but accurate perception — to see reality as it actually is. And reality, Joe insists, is that you already have far more resources than the lack-voice will admit: just having a screen to watch a video on puts you ahead of nearly every human in history. The two other classic sabotages follow the same logic: claiming "I don't have enough time" is simply time-poverty (a fine subject for gratitude itself), and turning the practice into a joyless chore means you haven't found the practice yet, since done right it feels better than a dopamine hit.

> "If you are not enjoying this, you have not figured out the practice."
