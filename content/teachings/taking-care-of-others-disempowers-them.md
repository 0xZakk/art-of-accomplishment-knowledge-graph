When you take responsibility for someone else's happiness, you are implicitly telling them they can't manage it themselves. Joe frames this as deeply disempowering — the equivalent of telling a person they're not capable of handling their own emotions or behavior, so you'll handle it for them.

> "To actually take responsibility for somebody else's happiness is to disempower them. Is to say, 'You're not capable of taking care of your own happiness, so I'll do it.'"

There's a cruel feedback loop here. The more you take care of someone this way, the more they come to see you as responsible — and the more they hold you responsible. So you end up shouldering a burden everyone agrees is yours, and you feel utterly alone in it.

> "The more you try to take care of somebody like that, the more that they will think that you're responsible for it and they will hold you responsible for it."

The antidote is to stop disempowering people and to apologize when you catch yourself doing it. You already know how bad it feels to be treated as incapable — that same helplessness is what you create in others when you manage their emotional state.
