The belief that keeps the inner critic in power is "I need it to be motivated — without it I'd just sit on the couch and drink beer." Joe attacks this head-on with an analogy. Imagine a boss who stands two feet from you and criticizes you every two seconds: do that wrong, be careful, don't forget this, you should also be doing that.

> "Most people would say, 'No, I'm quitting if I have a boss like that.' But somehow or another, I need my internal boss to act just like that."

Nobody would call that boss a recipe for great work — yet people insist their internal version is necessary. The truth is the opposite. When the critical voice slows down or goes quiet, you get a surge of energy and become more productive, because your adrenal system is no longer under constant attack, no longer cycling "I've done something wrong, I've done something wrong." The critic doesn't fuel performance; it taxes it.

> "When that voice starts changing, starts slowing down, even can go away... all of a sudden you have so much more energy because you're not constantly under attack."
