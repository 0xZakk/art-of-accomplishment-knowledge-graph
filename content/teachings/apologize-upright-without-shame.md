The second A is to apologize — but how you apologize determines whether it helps or hurts. Joe distinguishes the "upright" apology from the shame-laden one. An upright apology simply states: "I was doing this. This is not how I want to be anymore," and acknowledges the impact on the other person. It's empowered and brings a feeling of relief.

> "It's far better to apologize without any shame... There's actually quite a really good feeling to apologize in an upright way. You're just saying, 'Oh, this isn't how I want to be.' It's very empowering."

A shame-laden apology does the opposite. When you apologize from "I'm bad, there's something wrong with me," you stay inside the identity of being a screw-up — and that identity is precisely what holds the old behavior in place.

> "If you're in shame, then it's like, 'Oh, I'm bad. There's something wrong with me.' And you're still in the identity of that you're a screw up... that's just looping you right back into the old behavior."

The upright apology also includes genuine acknowledgment of what the behavior was like on the receiving end — "I acknowledge that this was shitty for you" — which builds empathy without collapsing into self-flagellation. The key is to feel everything that comes with the apology while staying out of shame.
