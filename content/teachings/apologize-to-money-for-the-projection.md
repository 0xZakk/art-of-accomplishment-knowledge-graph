Near the end of the session Joe does something unusual: he asks the woman to apologize to money for the projection she's put on it. She made money the carrier of selfishness, of unearned luck, of attention she couldn't have. Her apology cuts straight to it.

> "I'm sorry for making you the most important thing in my life."

That apology reframes the whole relationship. Money wasn't the villain or the prize; it was a screen onto which she projected her deepest, most painful material — being seen, being safe, being worthy. And precisely because it kept reflecting that material back, it functioned as a teacher.

> "What a teacher money has been. I mean, there's no way money could have taught you so much if it wasn't seeing you on some level or reacting to some truth in you."

The final move is to feel toward money the same easy, unearned being-seen she feels with her own family. When the projection drops, money stops being a battlefield and becomes a mirror — neutral, available, and quietly instructive.
