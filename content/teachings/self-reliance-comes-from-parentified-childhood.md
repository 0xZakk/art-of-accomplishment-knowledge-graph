Joe traces the entire self-reliance pattern back to one root: as a child you were taught to take care of your parents rather than be taken care of by them. It can look like keeping a parent happy, stopping their criticism, performing for approval, or doing their chores — but underneath, the lesson was the same: you were not going to be taken care of.

> "You as a child were taught that you needed to take care of your parents rather than your parents taking care of you."

The second, more insidious lesson is that you were to blame for it. A child can't survive the thought "no one is taking care of me," so instead of facing that, they conclude they're responsible and set out to take care of themselves. The unconscious bargain becomes: if I perform just right, if I do the right things, I'll be okay.

> "Instead, what you do is you just start learning to take care of yourself and telling yourself that you're to blame for not being taken care of... The pattern is that if you perform just right, if you do the right things, then you're going to be okay. And as it turns out, it doesn't freaking work."

Recognizing this origin reframes adult symptoms — responsibility for others, lost wants, authority issues, not asking for help — not as character flaws but as a child's survival strategy still running.
