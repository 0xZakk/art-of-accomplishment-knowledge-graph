Joe learned it the hard way in college bars: the people who got the deeper thing they wanted were not the needy, chasing ones — they were the self-possessed ones who felt good simply being with themselves. The chasers might get something short-term, but never the real thing underneath. The same dynamic plays out in business and families.

> "You'll often see that there's one person who's chasing and one person who's running. There's not two people standing upright."

When you stand upright in yourself — knowing that everything you need to be good with yourself is available right now, not in the future or past — you stop chasing, and you start attracting other people who are also upright in themselves. That, Joe says, is what makes life really satisfying. He paints the contrast inside a business meeting: one person scared of disappointment and judgment, the other unbothered.

> "Yeah, people are going to judge me and I still have me. Yeah, people are going to like me and I still have me. Yeah, I'm going to mess up and I still have me. I'm right here. I'm available right now."

The self-possessed person isn't immune to judgment; they simply don't lose themselves to it. That groundedness is magnetic precisely because it isn't performed for anyone.
