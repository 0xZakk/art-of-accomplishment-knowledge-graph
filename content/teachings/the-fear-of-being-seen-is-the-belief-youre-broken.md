At its root, the fear of being seen is not a fear of attention—it's a conviction that what's underneath, once exposed, will prove you defective. Joe describes two forms: an acute version (social anxiety, the person who is in the meeting but somehow unremembered) often trained in childhood, and a universal version everyone carries. Both rest on the same belief.

> "All of the fear of being seen is a belief that there's something inherently wrong with you. If they see me, they will find out that I am broken."

In the freeze moment, this fear stacks across three layers. The head self-corrects and insists "they don't like me." The emotional center fuses fear with shame. The nervous system drops into fight, flight, or freeze. Joe names the cruelest twist: you turn the external eye of judgment inward and become your own attacker, demanding why you can't just speak.

Because the fear is fundamentally about shame—"if they see me, they will find out that I am broken"—the work isn't to perform better. It's to address the underlying belief that there was ever anything broken to find.
