Compulsive helping has roots. Joe traces the helper mindset to a childhood in which your job was to take care of your parents emotionally, rather than the other way around — and when you failed to manage their emotions, you got in trouble.

> "When you're in the helper mindset, what has happened to you most likely is that you were in a relationship with your parents where your job was to help them emotionally rather than their job was helping you emotionally."

So the adult who helps everyone isn't being noble; they're running an old survival strategy of avoiding trouble. Maybe it shows up as guilt when you're not helping, maybe as a body-level fear of being hit or punished — but underneath is the conviction that "if I don't help them, I'm going to get in trouble."

Naming this with the people you over-help is part of the cure: "I feel guilty when I'm not helping you. Do you actually want me to feel that?" Most people don't want help born of obligation — Joe notes you wouldn't want someone at your party who only came because they felt obligated. And if someone genuinely does want your guilt-driven help, that relationship is worth questioning, with compassion for the pattern but clarity that it isn't healthy.
