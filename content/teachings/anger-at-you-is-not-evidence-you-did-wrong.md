When someone gets angry at you and you immediately feel you must have done something wrong, that reaction isn't logic — it's a childhood survival strategy. Joe explains that when a parent got angry, a child can't afford to conclude the parent is unsafe or crazy, because the child depends on that parent for care. So the child takes on the blame instead: "I must have done something wrong."

> "If they're angry at me, I must have done something wrong. It's not logical, but it's how we were taught."

That conclusion gets wired in and follows us into adulthood, where another person's anger automatically triggers the same self-blame even when we've done nothing wrong. Alongside it runs a second reaction in the nervous system — a sense of physical danger even when none exists. Joe is unambiguous that real physical danger is a different matter: if you might actually be hit or hurt, leave and call the police. But most anger isn't physical danger, and recognizing the old childhood pattern for what it is loosens its grip — the anger stops being a verdict on your worth.
