Joe latches onto the question from the Gospel of John that Theo raises — "Do you want to be healed?" — because it's the one most people skip. We assume the answer is obviously yes. I want to be rich, healed, different, free of guilt, free of jealousy. But we rarely stop to ask whether we actually want it, because healing means becoming someone different, with a different story.

> "Do you want to be healed? And that's a crazy question because if I get healed then I'm different. If somebody gets healed, they have a new story."

What Joe finds beautiful is that Theo doesn't speed-bump past the question. He wrestles with it, and in wrestling he sees that his old behaviors served him somehow — that there's a real fear in letting them go. That honesty is not an obstacle to transformation; it is the transformation beginning. Naming the part of you that doesn't want to change is more honest, and more effective, than pretending the whole of you is on board.

> "He's really wrestling with it... not only is he in the question, he also sees that the behavior of his past has been useful in some way."
