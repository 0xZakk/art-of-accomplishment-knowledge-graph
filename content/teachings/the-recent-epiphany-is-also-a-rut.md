When Theo recounts how he discovered he had no sense of self and delegated his worth to others, Joe notices something: the story is polished. It's automatic, not fresh. He's told it many times and isn't on his edge while telling it. That, Joe says, is the tell. In transformation there are two things hard to let go of — the old story of the distant past, and the recent epiphany of the very recent past.

> "Often times the way we think about it is that every epiphany becomes a future rut. That it is really good for the moment, but eventually it's another avenue to contain yourself by instead of finding who you really are."

The insight that once brought relief — "I get my self-worth from others" — is true, but it's only one of a thousand stories you could tell about the same childhood. Held tightly, it hardens into another container, another fixed identity. So creating a new story means releasing not just the ancient wound but the freshest realization about it. The epiphany was a doorway, not a destination.
