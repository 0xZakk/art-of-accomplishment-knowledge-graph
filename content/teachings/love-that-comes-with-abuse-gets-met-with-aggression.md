If love arrived alongside abuse in childhood, the nervous system wires the two together. So when love shows up later—especially love that's "too good"—it triggers a move toward abuse, because that's the only template the system has for what love is.

> "If love came with abuse, when they start to love, they're going to go towards abuse because it's what's wired in them."

Joe shows this twice in the Good Will Hunting scene. First, Will turns the abuse inward: he's furious at himself for breaking up with Skylar, beating himself up, repeating the abuse as self-abuse. Joe notes that "shame is anger turned inwards," and you can see it on Will's face. Second, when the therapist's love finally lands, Will's anger moves outward and he hits him—doing exactly what he learned love to be.

> "He does exactly what he learnt love to be. The anger moves outwards. He literally hits the therapist."

The therapist's refusal to change in response—keeping an open heart through the aggression—is what finally breaks the wiring.
