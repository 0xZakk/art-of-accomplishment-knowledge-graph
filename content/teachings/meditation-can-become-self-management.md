Joe is a longtime advocate of meditation, but he's candid that for years his own practice was a sophisticated way of managing himself. The teaching was "be with whatever is," yet he spent the sit constantly steering his experience—chasing expansion, fleeing worry, grading each moment against the last.

> "I was constantly in a management of my own experience."

The subtler trap is dissociation. Brett describes using meditation to "hold myself at some distance, some safe distance"—genuinely useful for becoming less reactive, but also a way to not notice discomfort by retreating to a place where there is none. Awakened access to peace can work the same way: a head awakening lets you bypass "all these fussy emotions" rather than feel them. The capacity to create distance can be a necessary step, but mistaking it for the destination keeps the unfelt material unfelt.

Joe's correction is to bring view and non-management to the cushion—vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder, and above all not trying to control anything. Done that way, meditation stops being hell and becomes "far more like hanging out on a beach and enjoying a nice breeze."

> "Typically it's not a management, it is an unwinding of management that allows us to enjoy the moment."
