The third A is to ask for help — and Joe frames it as "accountability like with love," not the punitive surveillance most people associate with the word. You ask people to remind you, to see you, to show you when the old pattern appears. This reflection accelerates change the way a teacher or a study partner does; trying to remember a new behavior alone, against an entrenched habit, is far weaker.

> "You're asking for accountability... but accountability like with love."

Being specific is essential. Joe describes asking exactly how he wants to be reminded. In one case, his pattern was rushing, and the team found that the precise antidote phrase — "I see how much you care" — instantly dissolved it, because the rushing came from a childhood belief that being late meant he didn't love people. Naming the exact words that neutralize a pattern requires understanding the pattern deeply, and that understanding is itself transformative. (His tennis-ball story — asking someone to throw a ball at him whenever he said "guys" — shows the playful, shame-free form this can take.)

Asking also breaks the isolation that keeps patterns alive. Hearing others say "yeah, I'll support you" reveals that your private reality isn't everyone's, that people want to help, and that you're not alone. And there's a subtler gift: by inviting people into a specific helpful action, you change their side of the dynamic too, freeing them from being a victim of your pattern.

> "It empowers everybody else around your pattern. So they don't have to feel a victim to it."
