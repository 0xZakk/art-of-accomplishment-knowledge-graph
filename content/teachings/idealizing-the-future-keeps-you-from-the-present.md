If you're caught in the stay-or-go question, Joe points out, you're living in the future — and the future is always either idealized or demonized. You leave the present in order to fantasize about a perfect state where you'll finally feel what you want, while the actual solution only exists in the present.

> "If you're in the question, you're in the future. You're trying to figure out the future. Because the solution only [exists] in the present."

This cuts both ways. You can idealize the partner or job and merge into it, then feel lost. Or you can idealize the freedom of leaving — "if I leave this, then I can be who I want to be" — projecting onto a future state the openness that's available right now. And idealizing the leave is dangerous because, without inner work, you simply repeat the pattern with a new partner who turns out just as controlling as the last.

> "Maybe I should leave this relationship so I can go find another relationship that's just like this relationship and be in this exact same pattern again in 2 months or 2 years or 2 decades."

The question worth asking is: how would the decision change if you let yourself be free, or open-hearted, right now — without doing the one thing you think you need to do first?
