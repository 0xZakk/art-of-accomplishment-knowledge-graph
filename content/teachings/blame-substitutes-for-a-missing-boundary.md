The reason we resist forgiving, the conversation argues, is that anger feels like the only thing keeping us safe: "I'm going to be angry at them so that I never put myself in that position again." The boundary is appropriate; the problem is that resentment has been recruited to do the boundary's job. The charge takes the place of the limit we never actually set.

> "The other thing that I'm hearing is sort of like it's almost like the anger or the resentment is taking the place of boundaries. So, once you have the boundaries, then you can let go of all this sort of charge."

Resentment held this way is corrosive — it feels protective but it eats at us long-term, releasing a toxicity that harms our own bodies and hearts. Tara describes seeing where she was responsible — not for the other person's behavior, but for failing to clearly state her boundary because she felt guilty about having one. Naming that gave her agency: she could set the limit directly and drop the blame entirely.

> "When I'm responsible, I can hold the boundary and just go, 'Yeah, I don't want that.' And then I don't need to blame the other person."

Once the boundary is real, the charge has nothing left to protect, and the heart can open again.
