The single biggest differentiator between people who thrive in uncertainty and those who collapse is ownership. People who say "this is an adventure I'm choosing to engage with" outperform those who say "this is being done to me" — in business, relationships, and personal transformation.

Joe uses the aviation metaphor: if your plane is crashing, you fly through the crash. You don't nose-dive because you've "realistically assessed" the situation. You keep flying, keep looking for options, and sometimes you're surprised to still be alive at the end.

Brett illustrates this with his brother's cancer diagnosis. His first response was: "There's a way we could have a deeper relationship by the end of your life because of this." That's not optimism — it's not pretending the cancer will be cured. It's ownership: I am choosing to make this an adventure rather than an oppression.

> "I am choosing not that I've chosen to be here, but I am choosing to take this as an adventure. I'm not choosing to take this as an oppression."

The distinction is subtle but critical: you didn't choose the circumstances, but you choose your orientation within them. This is responsibility without blame.
