Once the resistance pattern is visible, Joe poses a choice: keep playing the game his father taught him—resist the help, resist the love—or play the game he actually wanted as a child. The wanted game was simple: to be loved and seen.

> "What's the game you want to play? What's the relationship you want? >> I want a loving relationship. I want a receiving and giving."

The man's father can never give him that. So Joe, working within the man's faith, invites him to find it elsewhere—to let himself actually feel God's love right now, not as an intellectual statement ("God loves everybody") but as a felt experience. As the man recounts being chosen for the session after praying for it, Joe names what's happening: he's accepting love in the very moment, finally playing the game he always wanted.

> "I'm watching you actually accept his love right now. You're actually playing the game you wanted to play as a kid."

The practical consequence follows directly. If he stops resisting and accepts the help that's offered, the right people will actually be able to help him—or the ones he has will start delivering. Receiving love isn't separate from getting unstuck; it is the move that unsticks him.
