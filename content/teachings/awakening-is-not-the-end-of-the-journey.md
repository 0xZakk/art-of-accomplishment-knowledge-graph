People treat awakening as a finish line — the awakened version of you will finally be the great leader, the great parent, the enlightened person who has arrived. Joe pushes hard against this. Awakening is not an end state. It recedes into the background the way learning to walk does: at first there's tremendous excitement, "Oh my gosh, I'm walking," and eventually you walk around all the time without noticing. The search ends, but ordinary humanity continues.

> "Awakening is not some conclusion. It is just a part of the journey."

The people who recognize this soonest, Joe notices, tend to be those who have already done their trauma work. People who pursued awakening as a single goal — and who still carry unresolved trauma — are more likely to identify as "the awakened one" and use spacious awareness to escape what they haven't faced. The ups, downs, friction, and unlearned lessons of being human don't disappear; evolution continues, which means friction continues.

> "Show me an awakened person and let's put them in a 7-hour car trip with two kids yelling in the back and see how awake they are."

Whether it feels like a good end or a bad end, Joe insists, it is not the end. This also goes the other way: people who think they've "lost" their awakening haven't lost anything, because what awakened — the awareness that has always been there — never went anywhere.
