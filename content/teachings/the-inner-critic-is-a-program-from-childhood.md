Joe frames the inner critic not just as an inherited voice but as a piece of software you installed for a reason. You took on the voice of a parent or teacher because, as a child, you were trying to answer one question: how do I not get in trouble, how do I survive? Internalizing their criticism was a strategy that made sense when getting in trouble was genuinely dangerous to a small, dependent person.

> "You took on that voice... because you were trying to figure out, oh, how do I not get in trouble? How do I survive?"

The problem is the program is still running long after the conditions changed. You can't get in trouble the same way anymore; you're allowed to make mistakes now. Joe makes the point vivid: the voice has probably told you that you made 2,000 mistakes this year, and yet here you are, life pretty good — evidence the threat it's defending against is largely gone. Seeing the critic as an outdated survival program, rather than truth or identity, is what makes it possible to stop obeying it.

> "It is a program. And though it cares for you, though it's trying, it's not effective."
