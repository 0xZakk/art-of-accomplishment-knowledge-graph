Joe draws on Patty Wipfler's book *Listen* to make a claim about disconnection: when a child feels disconnected, there is usually a big emotion they aren't feeling. Let them feel it all the way and they come back into connection on their own. He and his family raised their kids this way—when a four- or five-year-old got ornery and oppositional, they'd make space and safety for the big emotion, the child would have a large expression, and the moment it passed: "Bump, hey, mom. Hey, dad," reconnected.

The same pattern holds for adults. Across thousands of people in his work, Joe sees that feeling and expressing emotion reliably restores connection.

> "They only feel disconnected when there was too much gunk in the system and they didn't get it all out."

The implication reverses our usual instinct. We suppress emotion to stay presentable and connected, but the suppression is what severs connection. The unfelt emotion sits between us like a wall. Expression—gentle, safe, complete—is what dissolves it. Joe adds a caution: when you first start expressing long-held emotions, they may get more intense for a while before they settle, which is part of the process rather than a sign it isn't working.
