One of the most direct exits from the fear of being seen is to turn toward yourself and ask, "What is it that I need?" This works because the fear runs on the opposite move—obsessively monitoring what others think of you. Opening your heart to your own needs flies directly in the face of that.

> "One of the ways to work on it is to actually open your heart to yourself and say, 'What is it that I need?' Because that flies exactly in the face of me worried about what you think."

Joe illustrates with a dinner guest—accomplished, loving, beautiful—who couldn't admit she had any needs of her own, only worries about her kids. That inability is the same pattern as the fear of being seen: an underlying shame that says it's not okay to have needs, so they get evacuated as if they were bad.

Caretaking everyone else is often a disguised attempt to get your own needs met by proxy—"if I take care of yours, then by proxy my needs might get taken care of"—except it never works. Naming your own need cuts straight through to the core of the pattern.
