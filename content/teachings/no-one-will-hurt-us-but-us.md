Joe rejects the common framing that AI is a thing that's going to hurt us. The danger isn't the technology; it's what we do with the time and abundance it creates, and whether our society can find a deeper sense of purpose in the gap.

> "I really don't like this framing that is AI is going to hurt us. No one's going to hurt us but us."

He points to the recurring historical pattern: societies destabilize when large numbers of people lose their sense of purpose. If we don't convert the freed-up time into something meaningful — into being more human — the same vacuum that breeds revolution opens up. The alternative framing he resists is the disempowered one, where "AI is occurring to me" and I just have to survive it. That stance, he says, is incredibly disempowering, because it hands away the very agency that determines the outcome.

Like raising a child, the AI transition can destroy you or become the best thing that ever happened to you — and which one it becomes is a function of how you meet it, not of the tool itself.

> "It can become the best thing that ever happened to you in your life, and you can grow because of it, and you can learn because of it, and you can be the person that you want to be because of it."
