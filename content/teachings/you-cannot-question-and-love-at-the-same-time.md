Joe borrows a phrase — "doubt is a sin of the heart" — to make a sharp distinction. To constantly question a relationship, you cannot have an open heart at the same time. The questioning itself requires you to stop loving.

> "Doubt is a sin of the heart... You have to stop loving somebody to question the relationship."

Crucially, this is not true of boundaries. Society tends to fear that loving makes you exploitable, but Joe argues the opposite: when you actually love, you have to draw the boundary, because to not hold it is to stop loving yourself and them.

> "You don't have to stop loving to draw the boundary. You don't have to stop loving to say this isn't working for me."

So the move isn't to question — it's to speak. When someone guilt-trips you, instead of silently doubting the relationship, you can say: "If I allow this, I'm going to close down to you, and I don't want to close down to you." Knowing in your bones that you can say that changes everything. Chronic questioning, by contrast, often hides a chasing dynamic — being the one who doubts means being the one who's pursued, a way of confirming you're wanted.
