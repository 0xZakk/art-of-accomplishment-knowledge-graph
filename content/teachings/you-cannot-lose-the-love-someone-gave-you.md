The widow fears that moving forward means losing her husband — that a new relationship will mean the photos come out of the frames and the love gets packed away. Joe flatly contradicts this. The love she received didn't leave when he died. It changed her permanently, and there is no mechanism by which she could ever undo that.

> "It's good news. You can't get rid of him."

He was the first person who really saw her, who caught every micro-expression and shift on her face. That experience of being attuned to didn't end — it taught her what love feels like, and she now carries that capacity in her body. The love isn't a possession she might misplace; it's a permanent alteration in who she is and how she can love.

> "You've been loved well."

Because the love is already inside her, the anxious project of "holding on" to him is unnecessary. You don't have to grip what you cannot lose. The fear of losing the love is itself the only thing creating the sense of a choice between past and future.
