The helper trap isn't a one-way street. Joe points out that the person being over-helped — the one feeling smug about the resentful helper — is fully part of the pattern too. They're accepting a surrogate of care instead of asking for the care they actually want.

> "You're getting a surrogate of care with this helping. Instead of asking for the care that you really want."

Rather than saying "this is what I really want from you," or "I'll get the job done if you treat me this way, and this is the care I need," they sacrifice their truth for close proximity to care. The result is a felt sense that "this person loves me" alongside a quiet feeling of being undermined and not standing up for yourself.

For the helper, the parallel move at the practical level — especially as a leader or parent — is to ask directly for the minimal information needed to feel safe, rather than hovering and helping to manage one's own anxiety. The whole shift is from getting care indirectly, through helping or being helped, to asking plainly for what you actually need. As Joe puts it, you move from "taking care of yourself by taking care of others" to "taking care of yourself by taking care of yourself, which means asking for stuff."
