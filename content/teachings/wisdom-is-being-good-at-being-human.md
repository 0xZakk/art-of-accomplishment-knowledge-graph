Each technological revolution changes which human capacity is most valuable. Joe traces the arc: physical strength before the Industrial Revolution, then learnable skills, then intelligence — and now, as intelligence gets outsourced to machines, wisdom. Wisdom isn't more knowledge; it's the quality of your decisions, your ability to see the patterns behind yourself and others, and your willingness to do the hard thing and feel the difficult thing.

> "You can hire someone to speak a language for you, but you can't hire someone to be yourself."

AI can write your emails and speak your languages, but it can't decide whether you'll scroll all day, whether you'll get addicted to it, or what kind of relationship you'll have with your mother. Those decisions — and the self-knowledge that informs them — are what's left when knowledge is free. If you can run a company with seventy free AI agents, the entire game becomes the decisions you make and the alignment you create, which is all wisdom. Joe's plainest definition collapses the whole thing into one phrase: wisdom is being really good at being human.

> "I would to some degree call wisdom being really good at being human."
