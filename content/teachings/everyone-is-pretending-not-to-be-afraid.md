Emile DeWeaver spent decades in environments — juvenile hall, county jail, prison — performing fearlessness. He believed he was the only one faking it, the only imposter among genuinely tough people. Then, as he and his peers grew into their 30s and 40s and began having honest conversations, a stunning revelation emerged: everyone had been pretending. Every single one of them was afraid, didn't want to fight, and thought they were the only fake.

Joe immediately draws the parallel to corporate boardrooms: "A whole bunch of people who are anxious and scared, doing everything they can not to show it, trying to have the bravado, and they're scared the whole thing's gonna go away."

> "Come to find out everyone was pretending. Everyone was pretending in the name of survival... not knowing we were all pretending."

The tragedy is that each person's isolation — believing they alone are the fraud — actually deepens the system's hold. When nobody breaks the pretense, the culture of fear perpetuates itself across generations.
