The fourth A is to act — and Joe is specific: find five places the pattern shows up and take clean contrary actions on all of them, as soon as possible. Five matters because one might go wrong, and because the common failure mode is taking a single scary action in the exact spot least likely to succeed (telling Mom she looks fat in those jeans). Spreading the action across five scenarios turns it into reps.

> "The most important thing about acting is you just have to find five ways to do it and you have to do it as soon as possible... five ways is important because one of them might go wrong."

Hunting for the five also deepens the work. The brain insists "I can only find one or two," but a real pattern shows up everywhere, and digging through the "gunk" to find all five makes the pattern clearer. Joe recommends starting with the easiest ones, where you'll be cleanest, and ideally doing them roughly simultaneously so you can read the responses and calibrate.

"Clean" means open-hearted — not hedging, not making excuses, not bracing for or fishing for an attack, just plainly doing the contrary action. Each action forces you to live through the feared emotional fantasy (rejection, attack, loss), and occasionally to actually feel the negative outcome, which teaches you to do the next one cleaner. Because you asked for grace in the ask, some messiness is expected; you simply take the next five and get cleaner.

> "Taking contrary behavior on five of them quickly is like doing reps. It's like, 'Oh, I know how to do this now.'"
