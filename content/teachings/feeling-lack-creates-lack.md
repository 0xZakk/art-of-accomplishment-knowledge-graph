Driving around LA, broke, Joe noticed that the billionaires he'd come to know also felt they didn't have enough — not enough money, love, or status. The realization landed instantly: his problem and a billionaire's problem were identical. Neither was about what they had.

> "The issue that I'm having isn't what I have, it's about what I think I have."

He was already in the top 10% of people on Earth by wealth, yet walking around feeling poor — while people with a billion dollars did the same. A daily verbal, viscerally felt gratitude practice with his wife shifted his perception, and within roughly six months he went from $40,000 in debt to money in the bank.

> "Me feeling like I'm in lack creates that lack. Me defining myself in lack at least supports that lack. Whether it's love, whether it's money, whether it's attention."

The mechanism is perceptual: defining yourself by what you don't have blinds you to opportunity. When Joe started seeing what he did have, he saw opportunity everywhere — noticing that twenty or thirty people had made money on even the paint lines on the road. Lack isn't a fact about the world; it's a thought process that reproduces itself.
