The man operates by a hidden rule: if he feels internal conflict, he isn't supposed to act. Frustration, a racing heart, agitation—any of it signals "not calm," which becomes a reason to hold back. Joe tests the rule by asking whether there was a single moment in the past year with zero internal conflict.

> "Can you tell me one minute one moment in the last year where there was zero internal conflict? ... No. ... But yet you've acted in the last year."

The rule is impossible to satisfy. There is always at least a low-level hum of conflict, so demanding its absence as a precondition for action guarantees paralysis—or constant self-policing. Joe points out the deeper irony: warning yourself multiple times a day that you're "not a calm person" is itself anything but calm.

The way out isn't to resolve the conflict first. It's to act, and even love, with a racing heart still going—to stop treating internal turbulence as a stop sign.
