The participant insists she's an unhappy person who hasn't felt good in years — yet she keeps smiling and admits she's actually happy in the conversation. Joe catches the contradiction: there's a persistent story in her head about being unhappy that overrides the moment-to-moment reality.

> "There's moments where you're happy, but you're like, 'No, I'm unhappy. I'm an unhappy person. This is what I do. I'm unhappy.' But then you're not noticing the actual moments of happiness."

The story persists because the feeling of unhappiness is so overwhelming that it drowns out the actual happy moments. The narrative becomes an identity — "this is what I do" — and the identity filters out the contradicting evidence happening in real time. Joe doesn't argue her out of the story; he simply points to the live data: a moment ago you weren't happy, you loved the painful part, and now you're happy.

The lesson generalizes: our self-concepts about being depressed, anxious, or broken can run as background narration even while the present moment offers something different. Noticing the actual moments — rather than believing the persistent story about ourselves — is where the shift happens.
