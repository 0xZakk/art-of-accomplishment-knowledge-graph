Authority issues in self-reliant people trace back to a parent whose authority couldn't be trusted — and that distrust gets replayed with every later authority figure. Joe notes that people with a problem with authority usually also have a hard time being an authority: they struggle to own what they actually know.

The practice is to take a breath each day and own something you know, with certainty, and without needing to convince anyone. Certainty here isn't arrogance; it's being upright in yourself rather than hedging and hesitating.

> "You're not trying to convince them because you're certain. Why would you have to convince them? You're not hesitating. You're not hedging well kind of maybe I know this. You're just upright in yourself."

The crucial addition is to do it with a big open heart. Owning your authority warmly teaches you that an authority doesn't have to be pushy or needy — it can simply be self-possessed. This rewrites the childhood template of what authority is.

> "An authority doesn't have to be pushy and doesn't have to be needy. An authority can actually just be upright in themselves."
