The woman carries a quiet rule: you're not good enough if you don't work hard. Joe tests it against his own life. He works a lot, but he wouldn't call it hard — and he's not even sure he'd call it work, because he loves it. The distinction matters. When you enjoy what you're doing, the category of "hard work" stops applying.

> "If you enjoy what you're doing, somehow that's not work."

This unhooks two things at once. First, the belief that money must come through hard labor — and therefore through suffering she's earned. Second, the deeper measure of worth attached to effort. If enjoyment dissolves "hard," then the moral credit she was trying to bank by working hard was never really about the work. It pointed back to something she had to earn as a child: attention.

Joe's reframe — "I want to enjoy my money" — lands as a small shock, because enjoyment had been quietly forbidden. The invitation is to let money, like work, be a place of pleasure rather than proof.
