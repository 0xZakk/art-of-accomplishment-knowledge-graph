The widow's instinct is that she must choose between her husband and a future — get rid of him to make room for something new. Joe reframes the dead husband not as an obstacle to move past but as a doorway. He's not what she has to let go of; he's the channel to the larger thing she's actually reaching for.

> "It's like he's the conduit to the greater thing that you're after."

He loved her so well that he taught her how to love. He was tender and nurturing in a way that softened her "guarded macho can't-show-that" defenses — so much so that she can hear his tone in her own voice when she speaks tenderly to her dog. The way he loved her became the way she loves. That makes him a living source inside her, not a closed chapter.

The relationship that loved you most isn't a finite resource you spend down. It's a conduit — a way the love can keep flowing outward into the world. Honoring it fully and letting it expand are the same act, not opposing ones.
