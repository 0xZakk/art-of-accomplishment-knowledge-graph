Holding an emotion down is not a mental act—it's muscular labor. Joe offers a live demonstration: try to stop all of your emotions right now. The discomfort you feel is the cost. Another word for that effort is stress.

> "Managing your emotion actually requires you to constrict your muscles, to be in pain, to be in discomfort."

Because the holding is physical, it leaves physical marks. Chronic suppressed anger shows up in the face, the voice, the mouth—"because we muscularly have to hold the thing that happens in our mind." Over the long term the bill comes due as depression, low energy, and a "lackluster life." Joe's own thirteen years without tears ended in a body that, once the crying finally came, felt transformed: "My hands feel different. My arms feel different. My head feels different," because the chronic holding had stopped.

The freed-up energy is the point. What was spent clamping down becomes available for living. When the holding releases, people don't just feel emotionally lighter—they report literally more energy and a different relationship to their own body.
