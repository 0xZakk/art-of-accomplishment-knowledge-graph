Joe asks whether the man had a parent who kept trying to steer him where they wanted him to go, someone he constantly resisted. The answer is immediate: "I resisted my dad all my life." With that, the whole present-day stuckness clicks into focus.

> "So, your marketers are your dad. I'm your dad right now. And you're resisting our help."

The pattern transfers. The man resisted his father, and that relationship never developed. Now he's swapped the father for life itself—and his marketers, and Joe—and resists them the same way, so nothing develops there either. The form is identical: I'm resisting you, even as I'm trying to get your love.

> "You were trying to get your dad's love the whole time, too. ... I'm resisting though I'm trying to get your love."

That double bind is the friction. It's also why Joe refuses to answer "how do I stop resisting?"—any instruction would just become one more thing to resist. As long as the helper is cast as the father, the help can't land. The way out isn't a technique but a different game entirely: choosing to accept love rather than resist it.
