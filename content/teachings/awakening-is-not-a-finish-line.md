One of the most common traps Joe sees is people treating awakening as an arrival. They access the peace, decide they're done, and get stuck. Because awakening is "one line of evolution"—not all of them—someone can wake up and still be running unhealed emotional, relational, and nervous-system patterns. The peace becomes a drug they reach for, and a new identity quietly forms around it: "I'm the awakened guy."

> "They've told themselves that this is a finish line and then they hit it and they think they're done. But they're not done. Evolution doesn't stop. It just keeps on going."

Joe's metaphor is walking. At first every step is momentous—"This is walking right here. This is walking"—and at first every flash of awakening is momentous too: "I'm awake. What the [—] is going on?" Ten years later it's just background; you hardly notice it, even as consciousness keeps expanding. Treating the initial experience as the destination is disruptive both before (you strive for it) and after (you stop evolving).

> "Every epiphany is a new rut."

The healthier framing is that awakening is natural and ongoing—as natural as learning to talk or walk—and there is no end to defend.
