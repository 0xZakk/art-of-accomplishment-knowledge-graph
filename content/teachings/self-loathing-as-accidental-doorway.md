Emile DeWeaver's second relationship with fear was enabled by an unexpected mechanism: he didn't care if he died. Having killed someone, he felt he deserved death. This indifference to personal survival freed him to do the right thing in prison even when it was dangerous — not from moral courage, but from self-loathing deep enough to eliminate the stakes.

Joe's reaction: "What an interesting way to turn guilt into freedom." The mechanism is fascinating but Emile is clear — "I wouldn't advocate that's the healthy way to deal with your guilt." He got lucky. The self-loathing opened a door that more constructive approaches might have eventually opened, but it got him doing the right thing even if for the wrong reasons.

The insight: sometimes doing the right thing for imperfect reasons shows you that more is possible. Emile wasn't acting from integrity — he was acting from indifference to his own life. But the results showed him a different way of being, which eventually matured into genuine courage and then into the collaborative relationship with fear he holds now.
