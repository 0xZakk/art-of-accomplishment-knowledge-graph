Most gratitude practices fail, in Joe's view, because they're done wrong: journaling lists or naming things over dinner stays in the head. The version that actually shifts your self-definition has three ingredients — it's emotionally felt, it's witnessed by another person, and it's sustained long enough to register.

> "It cannot be a intellectual I'm grateful for this thing. It is a felt sense of I'm grateful for. It has to be felt in your bones."

The felt part matters because big emotional experiences retrain us faster — the same reason boot camp changes behavior. The witnessed part matters because showing another person "this is who I am now" lands differently than telling yourself; sitting across from someone in an open body position, trading single-sentence gratitudes, lets mirror neurons carry each person deeper into the feeling. And the sustained part — seven to ten minutes, once or twice a day, over weeks — is what's required to compete with a lack-voice that runs constantly. To feel it at all, Joe adds simple somatic cues: chin down, jaw loose, body relaxed and unprotected.

> "Expressing gratitude, just like in a journal, is far less powerful than the way that we prescribe doing it, which is to do it with a deep-felt somatic sense of gratitude."
