For someone locked in the endurer pattern, the pain was never felt—only endured. Joe explains that anger is often the most reliable route through that armor and into the sadness waiting underneath.

> "When you go through that pattern, often times anger is a great way to move through that pattern into the sadness that's really there underneath, actually feeling the pain that you couldn't feel as a kid."

The reason the pain was buried in the first place is that it was unsurvivable for a child. To actually feel it then would have meant complete devastation—with no one to take care of you and no way to hold onto a sense of self.

> "If you did feel it as a kid, then it would just be like complete devastation. Who is there to take care of you? ... You're just going to be completely lost."

In the scene, Will's anger moves outward and he strikes the therapist; the therapist stays open, and that is the hinge where the endurance finally gives way to the long-avoided pain. The transformational moment is feeling, at last, what could never be felt before.
