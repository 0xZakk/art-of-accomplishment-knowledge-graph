Harry keeps catching himself shutting down an emotional expression — covering tears with a smile, then narrating how he'll phrase the feeling so it "sounds good." Joe names this directly: that ongoing calculation about how to present yourself so others understand and approve is judgment. It isn't a neutral social skill; it's the editor standing between you and your own natural expression.

When Harry insists he wasn't judging himself in that moment, Joe points out the mechanism in real time. The smile, the reformulation, the worry about being liked — each is the editor at work.

> "It's like seeing the thing and then it's like how am I going to express this to you and everyone in the way that sounds good and the way that they'll understand? — How is that not judgment?"

What makes it hard to see is that it's been running so long it feels like the self rather than a habit laid over the self. Harry says he's never not seen it there. Joe doesn't ask him to stop it by force; he asks him to notice it, because noticing is the first crack in the automatic loop.

> "I'm not judging you. That's all you in your head."
