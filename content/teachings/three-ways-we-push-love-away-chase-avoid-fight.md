Joe names three categories for how we push away the love we most want. We chase it — getting needy, looking for praise, demanding time and attention — which others instinctively recoil from. We avoid it — feeling that if we let love in we'll lose control, lose ourselves, be dominated or smothered, so we walk away or resist. Or we fight it — defending ourselves in a way that feels internally like self-protection but lands on the other person as a battle.

> "We're either avoiding it, we're either chasing it, or we're defending/fighting."

Each pattern traces to a childhood version of love. The avoidant was loved to the point of losing their own identity. The fighter learned that love always came with a fight. Joe's example is a father in a power struggle with his eight-year-old: he might win compliance or provoke rebellion, but he won't get love. Even the fighter is fighting to be seen and loved — and getting neither. Whichever shape it takes, the move is the same: wanting love and repelling it in the same gesture.

> "What you're fighting for is to be seen, to be loved. And you're not getting seen, and you're not getting loved. But you're also pushing away the love."
