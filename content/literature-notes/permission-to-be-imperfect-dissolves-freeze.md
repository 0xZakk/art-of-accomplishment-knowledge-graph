A young actress had stage fright so severe she couldn't go on — her understudy had to replace her multiple nights. Working with Tara, she discovered the root: she believed she had to be Meryl Streep at 24 years old. Not just perfection, but perfection beyond expectation. The moment she gave herself intellectual permission — "I'm allowed to not do this perfectly" — she could get on stage.

This extends beyond performance. The permissioning of "this could be a dumpster fire, and that's okay" is fundamentally different from wanting the dumpster fire. It's signing up for reality: mistakes will happen, I'm here for whatever comes, and I trust there's a reason.

The back end matters too: celebrating what happened — including what went wrong — shifts the entire learning loop. If mistakes are punished, you get more freeze next time. If mistakes are celebrated as learning, the nervous system registers that performing is safe. Tara runs exercises in her company where teams celebrate failures explicitly, because "if we're beating ourselves up from our mistakes, we're not going to get the wisdom."
