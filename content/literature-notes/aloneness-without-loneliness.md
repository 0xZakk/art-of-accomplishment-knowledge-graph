As self-discovery work deepens, a paradox emerges: you feel more connected to others yet more fundamentally alone. This isn't loneliness—it's recognizing the truth that nobody can share your experience with you. No matter how deeply you love, your experience remains yours alone.

Joe describes the deeper territory this opens into: identity becomes simultaneously more solid and more transparent. You feel a firmer sense of who you are, yet that identity is more fluid than ever. Eventually the question becomes "what am I?"—an unanswerable question that, when held long enough, produces the felt experience of being everything and nothing simultaneously. A piece of dust in infinite space, and all of space itself.

This connects to Brett's observation that we often conflate connection with clinging. When clinging falls away, we might mistake the absence of grasping for the absence of connection. The aloneness that remains isn't isolation—it's the ground from which authentic connection becomes possible.

> "The truth is that we're alone. Nobody can share your experience with you."

> "There's a bit of a paradox in it until it's lived, and then there's no paradox in it at all."
