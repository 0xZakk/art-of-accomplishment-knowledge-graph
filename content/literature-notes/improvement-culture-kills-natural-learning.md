Humans are genetically programmed to learn—babies smile most at sideways faces (play) rather than straight-on faces (feeding). Yet school systems that demand improvement and grades can kill this natural desire: 47% of highly intelligent kids fail high school. Research shows that telling a kid they're "smart" makes them stop trying after failure, because trying and failing threatens the identity.

Unschooled kids pulled from traumatizing school environments often do nothing for three to six months, then voluntarily begin learning and can cover fractions to calculus in a few months—because they *want* to learn.

This pattern extends to business. A Dutch nursing company (Buurtzorg) abandoned efficiency metrics and instead focused on helping patients become self-reliant—hooking into nurses' authentic desire to help people. They became 40-60% more efficient than competitors. A collections agency built on transparency and genuine connection outperforms traditional approaches.

Companies that hook into authentic human desires—altruism, self-interest, teamwork, play—massively outperform improvement cultures. Compartmentalizing your heart to do business makes you less efficient, because everyone you interact with compartmentalizes right back.
