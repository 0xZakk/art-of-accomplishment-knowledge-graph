The habenula is a brain structure that regulates dopamine and serotonin production. Its evolutionary purpose is to prevent repeated dangerous failures — telling a rat not to go down the same dead-end path, or telling a defeated lion not to challenge a stronger rival again. It suppresses reward signals when failure is perceived, reducing motivation to retry.

In modern humans, this mechanism becomes pathological. People who are depressed often have a habenula that perceives failure even before it happens, creating the classic depressive experience: "there's no way out, there's never going to be a way out, I'm never going to succeed, this is going to last forever." The biological mechanism designed to keep us safe becomes the prison.

> "People who are depressed often have like the habenula is not working in the same way — it's perceiving failure even before it happens. So then you don't even try."

This is why reframing failure as iteration isn't just a mindset trick — it's working directly against a neurobiological mechanism that otherwise shuts down effort and hope.
