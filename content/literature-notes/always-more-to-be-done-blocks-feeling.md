Ali Abdaal couldn't let himself celebrate hitting the New York Times bestseller list because "there's always more to be done." Joe connected this directly to Ali's childhood: growing up, Ali could never let his mom see him playing video games because there was always a chore, homework, or exam prep that should be happening instead.

The pattern transferred seamlessly from childhood to adulthood. The mother's voice became the inner voice: "I can't continue playing Baldur's Gate 3 because there's brainstorming to do about the next video or building a landing page." Joe pointed out the irony—Ali's goal was a "four-hour work week," but the internalized belief that rest is dangerous makes rest impossible regardless of external circumstances.

Joe drew the deeper connection: "I don't have time to feel because there's something that has to be done. I don't have time to be sad over this woman because I have to be successful." The compulsive productivity isn't about productivity at all—it's an emotional defense mechanism that prevents feeling. The busyness blocks the sadness, the grief, the gratitude—all the emotions that would require slowing down enough to notice them.
