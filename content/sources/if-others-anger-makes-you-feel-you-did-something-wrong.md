## Summary

Joe addresses a common difficulty: when someone gets angry at you, you feel you must have done something wrong. He opens with a story of a woman he coached who dealt with a famous Silicon Valley narcissist who yelled at her team every two weeks. Instead of bracing, she stepped toward him — acknowledging his vision, asking him not to yell so the team could help him more effectively. He stopped yelling at her team, and their relationship became so good he later joined the board of her next company. The reason: for the first time, he felt safe with her.

Joe breaks down the two reactions that make anger frightening. First, the childhood logic that "if they're angry at me, I must have done something wrong" — because the alternative, that the parent is unsafe, is unbearable for a child. Second, a nervous-system sense of physical danger even when none exists (and if there is real danger, Joe is clear: leave, call the cops). On the other side, the angry person is usually scared, feeling out of control and alone; their anger is an act of vulnerability and a bid for engagement, not a verdict on you.

The path through has two parts. First, get good with your own anger — like the woman who yelled in her car for ten minutes on her commute until anger stopped being scary — because until you can love your own anger you'll always fear others'. Second, with the other person, you can always draw a boundary (and should, returning in 20 minutes so they don't feel abandoned), and from that empowered place you can choose to step toward their anger, see the fear and aloneness underneath, and stop taking it on. To do that you have to stop seeing them as the oppressor, which means refusing to see yourself as the victim.
