## Summary

Joe argues that imposter syndrome can't be overcome by trying to convince yourself you're not an imposter — because the evidence actually supports the fact that you are one. Nobody doing anything of consequence knows exactly what they're doing. If you knew how to solve a problem, it wouldn't be a problem — it would be a to-do list. An expert is simply someone who has failed more than everyone else.

The key insight that destroys imposter syndrome isn't "I'm not an imposter" but "everyone is an imposter." When you ask "who isn't an imposter?" — Elon Musk didn't know how to build a spaceship, your favorite writer needed countless edits — the ground falls out, but so does the problem. If everyone is a kindergartener, being a kindergartener doesn't bother you.

Joe then provides a practical integration: imposter syndrome is really fear, and that fear is excitement without the breath. Saying "I'm excited" ten times can transform the felt experience from anxiety to energy.

## Key Quotes

> "If you think you're an imposter, it's because you are. Nobody who's doing anything important in the world knows how to do it."

> "The whole idea of an expert just means it's somebody who has not known how to do something for a very long period of time and kept on trying."

> "When you realize we're all imposters and that all that really matters is how we feel like we've lived our lives, imposter syndrome just can't matter at all."

> "What that fear actually is, is excitement without the breath."
