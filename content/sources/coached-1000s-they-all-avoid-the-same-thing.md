## Summary

Joe walks through the famous "it's not your fault" therapy scene from Good Will Hunting, using it to illustrate what nearly everyone he coaches avoids: feeling the pain they were never allowed to feel as children. He reads the scene moment by moment—the file, the hesitation, the trust the therapist has already built—and shows how each beat reveals shame. When someone doesn't want to look at something, Joe says, it means there's shame in their system, and that is exactly where the healing is.

The heart of the analysis is the "endurer pattern." Will got his only sense of self by resisting his abusive father, choosing the wrench "cuz [ __ ] him." That strategy—I can't be broken, I'll endure whatever you throw at me—becomes a way of relating to all of life, where everything good has to be fought for and love itself becomes something to be endured rather than received. Because love arrived alongside abuse, when love gets too good, Will moves toward abuse, including self-abuse.

Joe highlights two key moves by the therapist: refusing to be "speed bumped" (the "yeah, yeah, I know" deflection of love) and keeping an open heart even when Will lashes out. The anger moves outward, Will hits him, the therapist doesn't change—and that constancy is what finally cracks the pattern open. Underneath the endurance is the unfelt pain and devastation a child couldn't afford to feel, and feeling it at last is the transformational moment.
