## Summary

A widowed filmmaker comes to Joe asking how to "let go" so she can exit the phase of grief she's been in for a year and a half after her husband died of a long, unexpected sickness. She frames her dilemma as a binary: her life used to be about "us," and now it has to become about "me." She wants permission to step out of the safe place she built for herself into whatever comes next.

Joe dismantles the premise that letting go is required. Instead of asking how to stop doing her work "for us," he asks what she would do if she kept doing it for them. The answer is the same — tell stories, do passion projects, give the love she feels. Joe points out there is no way for her to lose this love; she literally cannot get rid of her husband. The photos aren't coming out of the frames.

The pivot is from holding the love personally to making it non-personal. Her husband taught her how to love by the way he attuned to her, by catching every micro-expression and shift on her face. The invitation is to love the world the way he loved her — to see and attune to everyone the way he saw and attuned to her. Joe guides her out of the story and into her body, asking her to make the love non-personal, then to feel both the personal love for him and the non-personal love at once. She lands in both at the same time and finds it beautiful.
