## Summary

In this conversation, Joe and his interlocutor unpack the question "should I stay or should I go?" — whether about a relationship or a job. Joe splits it into two buckets: the chronic questioner who has asked it through many relationships and jobs, and the person who's been content in something for years and only recently arrived at the question. They look the same on the surface but have different roots. Underneath both is a single question: can I stay with myself here, or will I leave myself? The chronic version is a prediction made in advance — "I can't stay with myself if I commit" — usually born of a childhood where commitment meant enmeshment, where you had to bend yourself to please a parent. The acute version is the felt sense of losing yourself into someone after the fact.

For the non-chronic case, Joe's core move is to ask: what happens if you show up uncompromisingly as yourself? Most of the time — 75 to 80% — it works itself out, because the person sees they'd lost themselves in some form of enmeshment. The failure mode is showing up spiky and resentful instead of free; real freedom (Joe cites Buckminster Fuller deciding to live exactly as he wanted) isn't a chip on the shoulder. Both leaving and staying can be idealized — projecting freedom or open-heartedness onto a future state that's actually available right now.

The deepest reframe is on commitment itself. You can't commit to staying together forever, because that requires abandoning yourself. The real commitment is to stay in connection — to neither enmesh nor abandon. "I'm not leaving you, and I'm not leaving myself." That commitment guarantees growth, because refining what works and what doesn't for you is the engine of intimacy and of knowing who you are. Finally, they land on doubt: "doubt is a sin of the heart." You have to stop loving to question a relationship — but you don't have to stop loving to draw a boundary. The closing of the heart is itself a boundary signal, pointing to a truth that needs to be spoken so the heart can stay open.
