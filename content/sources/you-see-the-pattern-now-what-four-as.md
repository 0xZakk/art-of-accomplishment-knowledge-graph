## Summary

In this conversation, Joe Hudson lays out a concrete method for changing behavior once you've already seen a pattern but your life hasn't changed. He calls it the four A's: announce, apologize, ask, and act. The method is for a specific situation — the moment of "Oh, I get it now" — and is most powerful for relational and interpersonal patterns rather than solo bad habits.

Joe illustrates with a real example from his own company. Returning from his August break, he recognized he'd been making excuses for people and doing parts of their jobs, which disempowered everyone. So he announced it to the entire company, apologized in an upright (non-shaming) way, asked everyone to remind him when they saw the old pattern and to have patience with the mess, and then immediately took five concrete contrary actions — clean emails to each person where the pattern had been showing up — all within the same day.

He walks through each step's nuances. Announce as widely as possible: it makes failure visible (which surfaces real emotion) and shifts the identity that others hold in place for you. Apologize upright, not from shame, because a shame-laden apology loops you right back into the old "I'm a screw-up" identity. Ask for help specifically — describe exactly how you want to be reminded — which creates loving accountability, breaks the isolation that keeps patterns alive, and invites the people around you into a different action too. Act by finding five places the pattern shows up and taking clean contrary actions quickly, starting with the easiest, doing reps.

The throughline Joe keeps returning to: you can do the four A's "rote" and they work okay, but they work powerfully when you feel everything that comes up while doing them — the grief, the fear of rejection, the decades of unfelt shame holding the habit in place. Crucially, the method only works after a genuine recognition, not from a "should." Forcing yourself through it as a boot camp, or applying it to morality you've adopted but not actually seen, makes it messy and ineffective. And it doesn't work for solo bad habits like smoking or exercise, which require stopping or repeating a behavior rather than taking immediate one-time relational actions. Done well, the four A's are a remarkably efficient use of willpower and a "joyful," if turbulent, way to front-load the emotional work that real change requires.
