## Summary

A man arrives feeling hopeless, even suicidal—stuck in the same financial, love, and life position for ten years. He frames it as needing help (with marketing, with overwhelm) while also wanting to help everyone else: his parents, his coaching clients. Joe locates the engine of the stagnation in that exact split: one part wants to be helped, another resists being helped, and the two pull in opposite directions, producing stuckness.

Rather than answer the man's "how do I move my life forward," Joe insists the thing to address is the very dynamic playing out in the room. The man keeps responding with "confusion"—I'm trying, I don't get it—and Joe reframes the confusion as resistance. The proof is in the pattern: every marketer he hired failed to deliver, which is how you know someone doesn't actually want help. Joe ties it to the man's father, whom he resisted his whole life while also trying to earn his love. He's transferred that game onto his marketers, onto Joe, and onto life itself.

Working within the man's Islamic faith, Joe uses the image of the bird that flies out to find the food God provides. The man admits he's told God he's too tired to fly. Joe guides him toward accepting love—from God, and from the people trying to help—rather than resisting it. The shift comes when the man feels, rather than intellectualizes, being seen and loved, and sees that if he stops resisting and accepts help, the right people will actually help him.
