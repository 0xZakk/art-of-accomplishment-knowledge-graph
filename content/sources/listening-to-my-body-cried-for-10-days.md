## Summary

In this lesson, Tara Healey — co-founder of the Art of Accomplishment and head of its teaching and coach training — walks through how to build somatic awareness, the practice of feeling what's happening in your body. She describes growing up as a "thinker," a straight-A brainiac who had closed her body off and lost touch with her emotions. Slowly, starting with Vipassana meditation and noticing simple sensations like tingling feet, she regained access to grief, anger, and fear she had cut off — and eventually to a deeper gut wisdom that her linear brain couldn't name but could follow.

Tara lays out a practical, graded approach. Start simply: bring attention to your hand, feel texture and temperature through the skin. Then move inward to the gut, taking an "elevator ride" down to the belly and just sensing. Then to the chest and heart, where old heartbreak might surface as tension. She demonstrates how attending to a hard conversation lets the body take its protective shape, soften, and "sequence" the held emotion to completion in seconds — so it isn't carried all day. The body, she explains, also points toward what we want: she felt an embodied urge to be "bigger" that turned out to be her wanting to lead larger groups, a knowing she reached through felt sense rather than logic.

She treats body awareness like going to the gym — short reps, lots of patience, made juicy and enjoyable rather than another chore. She troubleshoots the common sticking points: feeling nothing (which is itself somatic data to explore), getting flooded by the nervous system (slow down, soothe it), and getting flooded by emotion. On that last point she shares that when she first did this practice at a Vipassana retreat, she cried for ten days straight, sitting on a backlog of grief — and recommends balancing every stretch of grief with an equal stretch of self-care. Crucially, she honors choice: if you don't like what you feel, you're allowed to come back up to the head, and some people carry things in their shoulders their whole life and never need to process them. On the other side of staying with the discomfort, she promises, is a gold mine — peace, joy, intuition, and even bliss available in something as simple as the breath.
