## Summary

Joe lays out a complete model of where anxiety comes from and what to do about it. Most anxiety, he argues, comes from not doing the thing you know you want to be doing—and the rest comes from the moment you are doing it (stepping into a bigger room) but aren't feeling it all the way. Anxiety isn't a productivity tool; it shrinks the intellect and makes you less capable, even though the mind insists it needs the anxiety to perform.

The first move is physical: regulate the nervous system through enjoyment and getting into the body—walks, yoga, stretching, massage—because a dysregulated system can't learn the lessons anxiety is pointing to. The second move is psychological and emotional: notice where you're telling yourself you should do something you can't, where you're not doing what you know you should, where you're not being who you are, and where negative self-talk hammers you. Joe contrasts two equally hardworking CEOs—one stressed and exhausted, one not—and shows the difference is whether they act on what they tell themselves versus accumulating undone shoulds. Not being yourself creates an everlasting anxiety, because if you can get in trouble for being you at any moment, you're constantly under threat.

He then weighs short-term versus long-term consequences (the upset mom now versus a lifetime of a mother who never knows you) to make the authentic move obvious. Finally, Joe names the loophole: sometimes anxiety is hiding excitement. His friend's stage fright vanished when told to "fill the whole room"—the anxiety was actually the excitement of being asked into a bigger way of being. Saying "I'm excited" ten times can flip it. Getting out of anxiety isn't a quick breathing trick; it's the long work of bringing your life back into alignment.
