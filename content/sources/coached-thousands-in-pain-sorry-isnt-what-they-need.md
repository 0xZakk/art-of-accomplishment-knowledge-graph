## Summary

Joe narrates a clip from Theo Von's podcast in which a UFC fighter named Shawn shares severe childhood abuse — sleeping under the bed to protect his mother from his violent father, the chronic terror, the lack of anyone to depend on. Joe uses the footage as a live case study in how trauma is held in the body and how to actually be with someone in pain.

He maps two responses to overwhelming abuse: becoming the endurer ("you can't hurt me," total self-reliance) or becoming the perpetual victim who keeps recreating the abuse. Shawn took the endurer path — and that's exactly why he can't easily express hurt. Joe shows how Shawn "speed bumps" the unfelt emotions: the longing for his father's love, the anxiety, the wish to be seen. The detail that finally breaks Shawn down isn't the violence but the anxiety he felt as a child, and the ancillary pains — the mother who didn't protect him, the teacher who took his desk instead of asking why he was tired.

The teaching at the center is about what real support looks like. Telling a deeply enduring person "I'm sorry" or "you're okay" reads as weakness and makes them feel small — the one thing they can never allow. What they need is to be witnessed in their strength, met with presence and unconditional love: "I'm right here. You're rocking it." Joe also names the trauma cycle's cruelest twist: wanting to hurt someone because you were hurt makes you believe you're bad, which confirms the original story that something was wrong with you, which makes the trauma harder to process. The only way through is to feel all of it.
