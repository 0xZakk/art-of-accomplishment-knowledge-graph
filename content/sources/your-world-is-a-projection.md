## Summary

Joe Hudson lays out a comprehensive taxonomy of four types of projection. First, psychological projection — disowned parts of ourselves (positive or negative) that we see in others, visible whenever we're triggered or idolizing someone. Second, projection onto the world — childhood learning (from theta brainwave years) about what love, money, and authority mean, which we then recreate through attraction, manipulation, and selective evidence-gathering. Third, projection of self — how our internal self-relationship becomes the lens through which we interpret reality (a thief sees a world of thieves; a self-loving person sees love everywhere). Fourth, the projection of "I" — the foundational assumption that a separate self exists at all.

Joe explains how emotions interact with projections: big emotional experiences define our learning (this is why trauma and basic training both reshape identity), and the temptation is to suppress emotions to feel "in control," which leads to dissociation rather than freedom. The alternative is to learn to surf emotions, love them, and see them as road signs rather than causes. Brett offers a powerful analogy: like listing uses for a brick, seeing through projections opens up possibilities — both emotional and practical — that fixed projections obscure.

The episode addresses the fear that arises when seeing everything as projection ("the world becomes a kaleidoscope"), and Joe reframes this as freedom rather than loss of control. You can still act, still have purpose, still be productive — but with a light-hearted joy. The integration practice: identify what triggers you, what you admire, and ask "what's looking out behind your eyes?" — sitting in the question rather than seeking an answer.
