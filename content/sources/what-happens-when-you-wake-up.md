## Summary

In this long-form conversation, Brett Kistler and Joe Hudson tackle a subject the Art of Accomplishment podcast has circled for 150 episodes without naming directly: awakening. Joe defines it as a shift of identity from the personal to the universal—the thing Buddhists call awakening and Christians call Christ consciousness—and explains why it happens often in AOA's work even though it is never made a goal. He breaks the experience into a head awakening (seeing through the sense of "I" and through all thoughts), a heart awakening (seeing through emotions into boundless love and the recognition that what you hold isn't particularly yours), and a gut or nervous-system awakening (the felt sense that you are inherently safe, reached most reliably through pleasure).

Joe argues that making awakening a goal slows the process down, because striving is "a lot of I"—he recalls calling it "enlightenment porn for perfectionists." Worse, treating it as a finish line gets people stuck: they hit the peace, identify with being "the awakened one," and stop evolving. Every epiphany becomes a new rut. He shares his own first taste of it during a BASE jump off an 800-foot bridge, where his sense of self briefly crashed like a "blue screen of death," and connects awakening to near-death experiences, flow states, and even his brother's response to brain cancer.

A central warning runs through the conversation: you can be dysfunctional at any level of awakening. Gurus who start cults often have genuine access to non-duality but never handled their emotional, relational, and nervous-system material. Head awakening in particular lets people supersede their emotions, so they avoid the trauma work that would actually free them—going back into what feels like hell but turns out to be heaven. Joe and Brett also reframe meditation: it can become a sophisticated form of self-management and dissociation, and the antidote is non-management, view (vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder), and enjoyment. They close on the practical guidance for anyone in some version of awakening: be grateful for the moments it's there, savor them, and the rest happens naturally.
