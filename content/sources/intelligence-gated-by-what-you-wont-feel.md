## Summary

Joe argues that to triple your intelligence you have to stop thinking of intelligence as purely intellectual. In AOA's framing there are three intelligences — head, heart, and gut — and because they interact, working on one improves the others. He walks through each. Intellectual intelligence grows when you abandon black-and-white thinking and learn to see the truth and the falseness in every point of view; school trained us to find the one right answer, but real intelligence sees what's right and wrong about every answer, which reveals more solution sets.

Heart intelligence is emotional, and Joe defines it not as reading the room but as the capacity to understand and fall in love with your own emotions. Avoiding emotions shrinks empathy and, crucially, destroys decision-making — neurologically, removing the emotional center leaves people unable to decide anything. Most of our decisions are made to feel better or avoid feeling worse, so until you can welcome all your emotions, your available solutions stay severely limited. Nervous-system intelligence is about fight-or-flight: a hyper-aroused system does black-and-white thinking and imagines a "false end" (get fired, then homeless, then... nothing), which collapses access to intelligence. The way to settle it is pleasure — you can't be in fight-or-flight while taking pleasure in something as simple as the next breath.

Most people in our society operate almost entirely from the head, so simply gaining access to the heart and gut roughly triples their intelligence. Joe illustrates with a CEO of 2,000 employees whose self-reliance kept him from expressing his wants; once he let himself feel his wants, new options for goal-setting opened up and his whole company shifted — "not because he had an idea, but because he felt an emotion." He closes with a somatic trick: we have neural nets in the head, heart, and gut, and simply placing your attention in one of them lets you speak from a different intelligence.
