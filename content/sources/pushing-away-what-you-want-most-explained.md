## Summary

In this long-form conversation, Joe and his co-host map out the five reasons people push away the very love they most want. The first is attachment: as infants we seek loving attention, and when we can't get pure love, we settle for attention in whatever form works — anger, cuteness, being right, being valuable. Those attention-getting strategies follow us into adulthood, where they actively repel the love we're after. Jealousy is the perfect example: a desperate bid for love that drives the loved one away.

The second reason is "wired together, fired together" — whatever your parents paired with love (criticism, abuse, control) becomes what you recognize as love, so love without that grit feels foreign and unsafe. The third is identity: if you don't feel worthy, adoration is physically uncomfortable and gets rejected, and confirmation bias has you noticing the one way you're not loved over the twenty ways you are. The fourth is safety — your nervous system can only accept as much love as it can tolerate feeling safe, and safety itself can be wired to danger. The fifth is that love, a positive emotion, is hard to welcome unless you've learned to welcome all the others; love shines into the dark ocean and everything unloved rises to the surface, so without the capacity to feel anger, fear, and sadness, the love quickly turns frightening and you stop letting it in.

The back half turns practical. Joe lays out the antidotes: self-compassion, opening the heart in small reps, welcoming all emotions through emotional inquiry, and spending time in pleasure to relax the nervous system. He frames love as the meta-variable — the more love in your life, the faster all transformation and learning go. The conversation closes on the research that self-compassion predicts healthy relationships better than self-esteem, and on the caution that expansive love can be confused with sex, adrenaline, or adoration until it stabilizes in the system.
