## Summary

Joe Hudson reacts to an interview with golf's world number one — widely understood to be Scottie Scheffler — who openly admits that winning fills him for only a couple of minutes before the question becomes "now what?" Joe uses the clip to illustrate something he's watched happen again and again in his coaching: people reach the dream and discover it didn't fill the hole. He's met hundred-millionaires who are miserable, asking "wait, I thought this was supposed to fix it."

Joe draws out two layers. The first is the futility the golfer names — every win and every loss comes and goes, and the head immediately demands the next thing. Rather than treating this as bleak, Joe reframes it as freedom: if nothing lasts and you'll never get everything, you're free to simply enjoy the present moment. He cites the idea (which he attributes to Eckhart Tolle) that winning feels good precisely because for a minute or two you stop thinking about what you have to do next.

The deeper moment Joe says he looks for is when a high performer realizes nothing external will ever make them whole. That recognition is the doorway. Instead of chasing the next achievement to fill the empty space, you can turn toward the empty space itself — stop avoiding it, get curious about what's actually there, and fall in love with it. That, Joe says, is where the real transformation begins and the whole identity can shift.
