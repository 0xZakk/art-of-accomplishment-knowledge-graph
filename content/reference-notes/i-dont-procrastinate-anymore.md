## Summary

Joe Hudson explains how he eliminated procrastination from his life through understanding what it actually is. He defines procrastination as telling yourself you should do something and then not doing it — crucially requiring self-abuse to exist. Without self-criticism, what people call procrastination is simply prioritization.

He outlines three key shifts that ended his procrastination: first, recognizing that procrastination is emotional avoidance and finding ways to make the doing enjoyable; second, adopting an iterative mindset where the goal is continuous improvement rather than perfection on the first try; and third, learning to welcome judgment as feedback that helps you improve rather than something to fear.

The episode also explores how procrastination often contains wisdom — it can signal that your priorities are off or that you need creative incubation time. Joe traces the roots of procrastination to childhood experiences where natural impulses were overridden by external pressure and criticism, teaching children not to trust themselves. The solution, he argues, is deep self-listening — not the critical inner voice, but genuine attunement to what would make the work feel great and what needs to be done first.
